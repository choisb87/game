# Market Benchmark — Gravity Pulse

## Genre Context

Gravity Pulse sits in the **one-touch arcade** genre, competing with titles that
prioritize instant engagement, skill-based depth, and session brevity.

## Top Chart References

### 1. Flappy Bird (Dong Nguyen)
- **What worked**: Ultra-simple one-tap mechanic, brutal difficulty, viral "score sharing"
- **What to adopt**: Instant retry loop, zero tutorial friction, score as sole progression metric
- **What to avoid**: Unforgiving collision detection that feels unfair; no visual feedback on success

### 2. Geometry Dash (RobTop Games)
- **What worked**: Rhythm-based one-tap gameplay, strong visual identity, level variety, music sync
- **What to adopt**: Neon aesthetic language, sense of speed escalation, particle effects on actions
- **What to avoid**: Level-based structure requires content creation; our endless format is lower maintenance

### 3. Duet (Kumobius)
- **What worked**: Minimalist one-touch rotation mechanic, beautiful audiovisual design, premium pricing ($1.99)
- **What to adopt**: Premium paid model works for polished arcade games; minimalism as a selling point
- **What to avoid**: Narrative-heavy approach doesn't fit our quick-session design

### 4. Alto's Odyssey (Noodlecake Studios)
- **What worked**: Premium pricing ($4.99), zen mode, gorgeous procedural visuals, no ads
- **What to adopt**: "Respect the player" ethos — no manipulation, no interruption; visual polish as differentiator
- **What to avoid**: Slower pacing doesn't match our adrenaline-focused design

### 5. Super Hexagon (Terry Cavanagh)
- **What worked**: Extreme difficulty + instant retry + short sessions = addictive; premium pricing ($2.99)
- **What to adopt**: The "die fast, retry faster" loop; difficulty as the core content
- **What to avoid**: Can feel hostile to new players; we soften the opening with wider gaps

### 6. Crossy Road (Hipster Whale)
- **What worked**: Endless arcade format, collectible characters, free-to-play with optional cosmetics
- **What to adopt**: Endless format with progressive difficulty; simple visual language
- **What to avoid**: F2P with video ads model — we choose premium for flow preservation

## Key Takeaways

### What the top games share
1. **One mechanic, perfected**: Every top arcade game has exactly one core input
2. **Instant restart**: Death → retry in under 1 second
3. **Visual clarity**: The player always understands what killed them
4. **Progressive difficulty**: Early runs feel achievable, later runs feel aspirational
5. **Score as identity**: High scores become personal achievements

### What we adopt
- One-touch core mechanic with deep timing skill curve
- Sub-second death-to-retry loop
- Clear collision feedback (particles, screen shake, color change)
- Speed + gap scaling that creates natural difficulty progression
- Persistent high score as primary motivator

### What we deliberately avoid
- Ad-supported monetization (breaks flow state)
- Energy/lives systems (artificial friction)
- Social leaderboards at launch (complexity without clear ROI)
- Multiple game modes at launch (focus on perfecting one experience)
- Tutorial screens (the game teaches itself through play)
